Research Interests

Symmetry-aware deep learning for biomedical imaging — building group-equivariant neural networks for medical image segmentation, classification, and computer-aided diagnosis.

🧮

Geometric & Group-Equivariant Deep Learning

Medical images have no canonical orientation — a tumor or cell looks equally valid rotated by any angle. Standard CNNs must learn each orientation separately, wasting capacity and data. My core research develops Mod-SE(2) networks built on the special Euclidean group SE(2), so that rotation and translation symmetry is baked into the architecture rather than learned, yielding more data-efficient and robust models.

The SE(2) group describes all rotations and translations in the plane. By designing convolutions that are equivariant to this group, the network's response transforms predictably when the input is rotated — instead of needing thousands of augmented examples to approximate that property.

I have extended this idea into a family of architectures: Mod-SE(2) for joint classification and segmentation, Mod-Seg-SE(2) specialized for segmentation, Mod-Cls-SE(2) for grading tasks with a feedback agent, and Cell Mod-SE(2) for multi-class microscopy analysis. Each adapts the equivariant backbone to a different clinical problem.

Medical Image Segmentation

Delineating exactly where a structure lies — a brain tumor boundary, individual blood cells — is foundational to quantitative diagnosis. My research designs symmetry-aware segmentation networks and benchmarks established architectures to understand which design choices matter most for clinical accuracy.

For MRI brain tumor segmentation, I developed the Mod-Seg-SE(2) framework, which exploits rotational symmetry to segment tumor regions more reliably across orientations.

To ground these designs, I also ran a benchmark study of DeepLabV3+, U-Net++, and Attention U-Net for blood cell segmentation, comparing how different encoder-decoder strategies handle small, densely packed structures.

Diagnostic Classification & Grading

Beyond locating structures, clinicians need a label: which type of lesion, what severity, what grade. My research builds classification and grading models for pancreatic cystic lesions, Alzheimer severity, and endoscopic disease scoring — often with an emphasis on interpretability and clinical workflow fit.

Much of my early work centered on pancreatic cystic lesions, progressing from VGG19 and patch-based segmentation through ResNet to hybrid vision transformers, building toward deep-learning-enabled computer-aided diagnosis (CAD).

More recently, the Mod-Cls-SE(2) network with a feedback agent targets Mayo Endoscopic Subscore grading — framing disease severity scoring as a structured, feedback-driven task.

Biomedical Imaging & AI for Clinical Support

Cutting across all my work is a commitment to AI that supports real clinical decisions — spanning MRI, microscopy, endoscopy, and CT-derived imaging. This thrust focuses on translating modern architectures into computer-aided diagnosis pipelines that clinicians can use and trust.

This research connects multiple imaging modalities under one goal: decision support. From phase-contrast microscopy and MRI to endoscopic imaging, I study how to adapt vision transformers, CAD frameworks, and equivariant networks to each modality's constraints.

The through-line is practical impact — methods designed not just for benchmark accuracy but for the workflows in which radiologists, pathologists, and clinicians actually operate.
